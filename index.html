Grade Field Guide

The USDA quality-grade ladder, the marbling behind it, and how the drill scores you — all on one card.

Quality Grade Ladder — A Maturity

  1. High PrimePrime
  2. Average PrimePrime
  3. Low PrimePrime
  4. High ChoiceChoice
  5. Average ChoiceChoice
  6. Low ChoiceChoice
  7. High SelectSelect
  8. Low SelectSelect
  9. StandardStandard

Nine callable grades, best to worst. The heavy rules mark family boundaries — the Choice / Select line especially is where contests are won and lost. One subdivision (High → Average → Low) is one-third of a grade.

Marbling Degrees → Grades

Within A maturity, the marbling score in the ribeye sets the quality grade. Learn these pairings cold:

Abundant High Prime
Moderately Abundant Average Prime
Slightly Abundant Low Prime
Moderate High Choice
Modest Average Choice
Small Low Choice
Slight¹ Select
Traces Standard
Practically Devoid Standard

¹ Select splits on the upper/lower half of Slight: Slight 50–100 grades High Select, Slight 0–50 grades Low Select.

A Word on Maturity

Quality grade is marbling plus maturity. This drill assumes A maturity (roughly 9–30 months — nearly all fed cattle) so every carcass grades straight off its marbling. As skeletal and lean maturity advance past A, the same marbling earns a lower grade, and the oldest carcasses fall to the mature grades (Commercial, Utility) that this drill leaves out.

How the Drill Scores

  • 10 Exact match
  • 9 ⅓ grade off (1 subdivision)
  • 7 ⅔ grade off (2 subdivisions)
  • 4 1 full grade off (3 subdivisions)
  • 0 More than 1 full grade off

One subdivision = one-third of a grade. High Choice → Low Prime counts as 1 subdivision off (9 pts) — the scale cares about distance, not family lines.
